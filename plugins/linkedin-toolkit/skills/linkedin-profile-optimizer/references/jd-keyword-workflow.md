# Target-JD Keyword Workflow

Recruiter search on LinkedIn is largely Boolean keyword matching. The reliable way to rank for the roles you want is to **use the same language those job descriptions use**, woven truthfully across the profile.

This workflow turns "match the JD" into a repeatable extraction → frequency → mapping → truthfulness sequence.

## Step 1: Collect 3-5 target JDs

- Pick **3-5 currently-open job postings** that closely match your target role and seniority
- Choose from companies you'd genuinely apply to — not aspirational moonshots whose JDs reflect a different talent bar
- Save each JD as plain text (LinkedIn job pages, company careers pages) so you can search/grep it later

If your search is broad (e.g., "any senior backend role"), pick variety: one infra-heavy, one product-heavy, one platform-heavy. The intersection of their language is what most recruiters will search.

## Step 2: Extract terms

For each JD, extract by category:

| Category | Examples |
| --- | --- |
| **Title + seniority** | "Senior Software Engineer", "Staff", "Principal", "Lead", "Engineering Manager" |
| **Hard skills** | Languages, frameworks, tools: Python, Go, Rust, TypeScript, React, Kafka, Terraform, Kubernetes |
| **Domain terms** | "B2B SaaS", "fintech payments", "healthcare data", "developer tooling" |
| **Responsibility verbs** | "designed", "shipped", "owned", "led", "scaled", "migrated" |
| **System / scale signals** | "distributed systems", "high-throughput", "multi-tenant", "low-latency", "5M+ users" |
| **Process / methodology** | "on-call rotation", "RFC process", "post-incident review", "design reviews" |

Skip purely generic filler ("strong communicator", "passionate") — those don't differentiate and aren't search-weighted.

## Step 3: Frequency + weight

Count how many JDs (out of 3-5) each extracted term appears in:

| Frequency | Treat as |
| --- | --- |
| 4-5 of 5 (or 3-3 of 3) | **Must-include** in headline + Skills + at least one Experience bullet |
| 2-3 of 5 (or 2 of 3) | **Strong-to-include** — fit into About specialties or Skills |
| 1 of 5 | Skip unless it's a personal differentiator |

This frequency check is what stops you from chasing one outlier JD's pet terms.

## Step 4: Map to profile sections

For each must-include term, decide where it lives:

| Profile section | What to include |
| --- | --- |
| **Headline** | 3-5 must-include terms across role + industry + signal keywords (within ~220 chars) |
| **About** | 5-7 must-include terms woven through the 7-step structure; 3-5 strong-to-include in the Specialties line |
| **Skills** | Every must-include and strong-to-include term that genuinely matches your experience (up to the 50 cap) |
| **Experience** | Weave terms into bullets for the roles where the work actually happened — don't backfill |
| **Featured** | Title slots using must-include role-specific language ("Backend platform redesign", not "My work") |

## Step 5: Truthfulness check

This is the step most candidates skip and it's how profiles get flagged in screens or interviews.

- **Don't claim skills you don't have.** Recruiters skim, but interviewers probe. A "Kafka" claim with no Kafka project will surface in 10 minutes of conversation.
- **Don't put terms in Experience bullets for roles where the work wasn't done.** If you didn't do RAG at Company A, don't put RAG in Company A's bullets — put it in your side project Featured slot instead.
- **Use exact JD phrasing where it's natural.** Forced phrases ("results-driven distributed-systems leader") read worse than the raw skill.
- **Cap the must-include density.** Stuffing 20 keywords into a headline trips both reader skepticism and LinkedIn's spam heuristics.

## Step 6: Cross-check before publishing

Quick checklist before saving the profile:

- Headline: top 3 must-include terms present in the first 100 chars (mobile-visible)
- About: hook (first ~265 chars) contains 1-2 must-include terms
- Skills: every must-include term that's truthful is listed; top 3 pinned are JD-aligned, not legacy
- Experience: at least one bullet per recent role contains a must-include term in context
- Featured: at least one item's title contains a must-include term

If a must-include term doesn't fit anywhere truthfully, that's a signal to upskill before applying — not to lie on the profile.
