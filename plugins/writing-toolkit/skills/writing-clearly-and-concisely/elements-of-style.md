## I. Introductory

Compact plain-English style guide: usage, punctuation, composition, concision, and word choice. Master these defaults; use good authors and local house style for finer points.

## II. Elementary Rules Of Usage

### Rule 1. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's.

Default: add `'s`, whatever final consonant.

- Charles's friend
- Burns's poems
- the witch's malice

Exceptions: ancient names ending in *-es*/*-is*, *Jesus'*, and fixed phrases like *for conscience' sake* / *for righteousness' sake*. For forms like *Achilles' heel*, *Moses' laws*, *Isis' temple*, recast when clearer: *the heel of Achilles*.

Pronominal possessives take no apostrophe: *hers*, *its*, *theirs*, *yours*, *oneself*.

### Rule 2. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last.

Use serial comma:

- red, white, and blue
- gold, silver, or copper
- He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents.

Business-firm names often omit last comma: *Brown, Shipley & Co.*

### Rule 3. Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas.

Set off interruption with paired commas. If interruption is slight, omit both; never use only one.

Correct:

- The best way to see a country, unless you are pressed for time, is to travel on foot.
- He saw us coming, and unaware that we had learned of his treachery, greeted us with a smile.

Always parenthetic:

- Year in a date; day of month after day of week: *April 6, 1917.* / *Monday, November 11, 1918.*
- *etc.* and *jr.*
- Non-restrictive relative clauses.

Non-restrictive clause adds supplemental info:

- The audience, which had at first been indifferent, became more interested.

Restrictive clause identifies the noun; no commas:

- The candidate who best meets these requirements will obtain the place.

Similar rule: set off introductory/final phrases or dependent clauses when they interrupt flow. Do not set off independent clauses with commas; see Rule 5.

### Rule 4. Place a comma before a conjunction introducing a co-ordinate clause.

Use comma before conjunction joining independent clauses:

- The early records of the city have disappeared, and the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed.
- The situation is perilous, but there is still one chance of escape.

Loose sentences are acceptable, but too many create monotony; see Rule 14. Often clearer: subordinate the relation.

- As the early records of the city have disappeared, the story of its first years can no longer be reconstructed.
- Although the situation is perilous, there is still one chance of escape.

Also use comma before *as* (because), *for*, *or*, *nor*, and *while* (and at same time) when they introduce second coordinate member.

If second clause begins with adverb (*accordingly*, *besides*, *then*, *therefore*, *thus*), use semicolon, not comma. Avoid colloquial *so* in formal writing; often recast with *as*/*since*.

If a dependent clause or introductory phrase comes before second independent clause, no comma after conjunction:

- The situation is perilous, but if we act promptly, there is still one chance.

Same subject, expressed once: comma before *but*; omit comma before *and* if relation is close.

- I have heard his arguments, but am still unconvinced.
- He has had several years' experience and is thoroughly competent.

### Rule 5. Do not join independent clauses by a comma.

Independent clauses without conjunction need semicolon or period, not comma.

- Stevenson's romances are entertaining; they are full of exciting adventures.
- It is nearly half past five. We cannot reach town before dark.

With conjunction, use comma:

- Stevenson's romances are entertaining, for they are full of exciting adventures.

Semicolon often shows close cause/consequence more forcefully than two sentences or a conjunction.

Adverb connectors still need semicolon:

- The roads were icy; therefore, we delayed the trip.

Exceptions:

- Very short parallel clauses: *Man proposes, God disposes.*
- Colloquial/dialogue forms: *I hardly knew him, he was so changed.*

### Rule 6. Do not break sentences in two.

Do not use periods where commas are needed.

Faulty:

- I met them on a Cunard liner several years ago. Coming home from Liverpool to New York.
- He was an interesting talker. A man who had traveled all over the world.

Fix by joining fragment to sentence. Deliberate emphatic fragments are allowed when clearly intentional:

- Again and again he called out. No reply.

Rules 3-6 cover core punctuation of ordinary sentences; master until automatic.

### Rule 7. A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.

Intro participle must modify sentence subject.

- Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman accompanied by two children.

Here *walking* modifies *he*, not woman. Recast if woman was walking.

Faulty -> better:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| On arriving in Chicago, his friends met him at the station. | When he arrived in Chicago, his friends met him at the station. |
| A soldier of proved valor, they entrusted him with the defence of the city. | A soldier of proved valor, he was entrusted with the defence of the city. |
| Young and inexperienced, the task seemed easy to me. | Young and inexperienced, I thought the task easy. |
| Without a friend to counsel him, the temptation proved irresistible. | Without a friend to counsel him, he found the temptation irresistible. |

Violations are often comic:

- Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap.
- Wondering irresolutely what to do next, the clock struck twelve.

## III. Elementary Principles Of Composition

### Rule 8. Make the paragraph the unit of composition: one paragraph to each topic.

Short subjects may need one paragraph. Longer subjects need topic subdivision; each topic gets its own paragraph so reader sees each step.

Possible outlines:

- Book/poem notice: account of work; critical discussion.
- Literature report: composition/publication; kind/form; subject; treatment; chief distinction; author characteristics; relation to other works.
- Novel: setting; plot; characters; purpose.
- Historical event: causes; event; consequences.

Avoid single-sentence paragraphs except transitions, textbooks, guidebooks, and brief-topic formats. In dialogue, each speaker change starts a new paragraph.

### Rule 9. As a rule, begin each paragraph with a topic sentence, end it in conformity with the beginning.

Best default for exposition/argument:

1. Topic sentence at/near beginning.
2. Middle explains, proves, develops, or illustrates it.
3. Final sentence emphasizes topic or states consequence.

Do not end with digression or minor detail. If relation to prior section needs setup, use a transition word/phrase; if transition needs multiple sentences, make separate paragraph.

Develop a topic by restating, defining, denying contrary, giving examples, proving, or showing implications. In long paragraphs, combine methods.

Narration/description may open with concise broad statement:

- The breeze served us admirably.
- The campaign opened with a series of reverses.

Animated narrative may omit formal topic sentences; paragraph breaks create rhetorical pauses.

### Rule 10. Use the active voice.

Active is usually direct, bold, concise.

- I shall always remember my first visit to Boston.
- Not: My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me.

Passive is sometimes convenient/necessary when topic or emphasis requires it:

- The dramatists of the Restoration are little esteemed to-day.
- Modern readers have little esteem for the dramatists of the Restoration.

Avoid one passive depending on another:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| Gold was not allowed to be exported. | The export of gold was prohibited. |
| He has been proved to have been seen entering the building. | It has been proved that he was seen to enter the building. |

Avoid weak passive nouns that contain the action:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| A survey of this region was made in 1900. | This region was surveyed in 1900. |
| Mobilization of the army was rapidly effected. | The army was rapidly mobilized. |
| Confirmation of these reports cannot be obtained. | These reports cannot be confirmed. |

Replace lifeless *there is* / *could be heard* constructions with real verbs:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground. | Dead leaves covered the ground. |
| The sound of a guitar somewhere in the house could be heard. | Somewhere in the house a guitar hummed sleepily. |
| The reason that he left college was that his health became impaired. | Failing health compelled him to leave college. |
| It was not long before he was very sorry that he had said what he had. | He soon repented his words. |

### Rule 11. Put statements in positive form.

Make definite assertions. Use *not* for denial/contrast, not evasion.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| He was not very often on time. | He usually came late. |
| He did not think that studying Latin was much use. | He thought the study of Latin useless. |
| not honest | dishonest |
| not important | trifling |
| did not remember | forgot |
| did not pay any attention to | ignored |
| did not have much confidence in | distrusted |

Negative antithesis can be strong:

- Not charity, but simple justice.
- Not that I loved Caesar less, but Rome the more.

Negative words other than *not* may be strong: *The sun never sets upon the British flag.*

### Rule 12. Use definite, specific, concrete language.

Prefer specific over general, definite over vague, concrete over abstract.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| A period of unfavorable weather set in. | It rained every day for a week. |
| He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward. | He grinned as he pocketed the coin. |
| There is a general agreement among those who have enjoyed the experience that surf-riding is productive of great exhilaration. | All who have tried surf-riding agree that it is most exhilarating. |

Specific detail makes prose vivid. Do not give every detail; give significant details concretely. In exposition/argument, state general principles with particular instances.

Spencer example:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| In proportion as the manners, customs, and amusements of a nation are cruel and barbarous, the regulations of their penal code will be severe. | In proportion as men delight in battles, bull-fights, and combats of gladiators, will they punish by hanging, burning, and the rack. |

### Rule 13. Omit needless words.

Vigorous writing is concise. Not every sentence must be short; every word must tell.

Common cuts:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| the question as to whether | whether |
| there is no doubt but that | no doubt |
| used for fuel purposes | used for fuel |
| he is a man who | he |
| in a hasty manner | hastily |
| this is a subject which | this subject |
| His story is a strange one. | His story is strange. |

Cut *the fact that*:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| owing to the fact that | since / because |
| in spite of the fact that | though / although |
| call your attention to the fact that | remind you / notify you |
| I was unaware of the fact that | I did not know |
| the fact that he had not succeeded | his failure |
| the fact that I had arrived | my arrival |

Often delete *who is* / *which was*:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| His brother, who is a member of the same firm | His brother, a member of the same firm |
| Trafalgar, which was Nelson's last battle | Trafalgar, Nelson's last battle |

Combine step-by-step sentences when one sentence better expresses one complex idea.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| Macbeth was very ambitious. This led him to wish to become king of Scotland. The witches told him that this wish of his would come true. The king of Scotland at this time was Duncan. Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth murdered Duncan. He was thus enabled to succeed Duncan as king. | Encouraged by his wife, Macbeth achieved his ambition and realized the prediction of the witches by murdering Duncan and becoming king of Scotland in his place. |

### Rule 14. Avoid a succession of loose sentences

Avoid a paragraph made of repeated two-clause loose sentences joined by *and*, *but*, *so*, or non-restrictive *who/which/when/where/while*. One is fine; a series becomes sing-song.

If you see a run of this pattern, recast some as simple sentences, semicolon-linked clauses, periodic sentences, or three-clause sentences, according to actual thought relation.

### Rule 15. Express co-ordinate ideas in similar form.

Parallel construction: similar content/function should use similar form. It helps reader recognize likeness. Do not vary form merely to avoid repetition.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method, while now the laboratory method is employed. | Formerly, science was taught by the textbook method; now it is taught by the laboratory method. |

Articles/prepositions in series: use once before first term or repeat before each term.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| The French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese | The French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese |
| In spring, summer, or in winter | In spring, summer, or winter / In spring, in summer, or in winter |

Correlatives (*both/and*, *not/but*, *not only/but also*, *either/or*, *first/second/third*) need same grammatical construction.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| It was both a long ceremony and very tedious. | The ceremony was both long and tedious. |
| A time not for words, but action. | A time not for words, but for action. |
| Either you must grant his request or incur his ill will. | You must either grant his request or incur his ill will. |
| My objections are, first, the injustice of the measure; second, that it is unconstitutional. | My objections are, first, that the measure is unjust; second, that it is unconstitutional. |

For many similar ideas, group them; otherwise use a table.

### Rule 16. Keep related words together.

Word position shows relation. Keep related words close; separate unrelated words.

Subject and main verb usually should not be split by movable phrase/clause:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| Wordsworth, in the fifth book of The Excursion, gives a minute description of this church. | In the fifth book of The Excursion, Wordsworth gives a minute description of this church. |
| Cast iron, when treated in a Bessemer converter, is changed into steel. | By treatment in a Bessemer converter, cast iron is changed into steel. |

Relative pronoun should usually follow antecedent:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| There was a look in his eye that boded mischief. | In his eye was a look that boded mischief. |
| He wrote three articles about his adventures in Spain, which were published in Harper's Magazine. | He published in Harper's Magazine three articles about his adventures in Spain. |
| This is a portrait of Benjamin Harrison, grandson of William Henry Harrison, who became President in 1889. | This is a portrait of Benjamin Harrison, grandson of William Henry Harrison. He became President in 1889. |

Modifiers belong next to words they modify:

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| All the members were not present. | Not all the members were present. |
| He only found two mistakes. | He found only two mistakes. |
| Major R. E. Joyce will give a lecture on Tuesday evening in Bailey Hall, to which the public is invited, on "My Experiences in Mesopotamia" at eight P. M. | On Tuesday evening at eight P. M., Major R. E. Joyce will give in Bailey Hall a lecture on "My Experiences in Mesopotamia." The public is invited. |

### Rule 17. In summaries, keep to one tense.

Drama summaries: use present. Poem/story/novel summaries: present preferred; past allowed if consistent. If present summary refers to earlier action, use perfect; if past summary, use past perfect.

Indirect discourse/question keeps original past tense:

- The Friar confesses that it was he who married them.

Avoid repeated tags in summaries: *he said*, *he stated*, *the speaker added*, *the author also thinks*. State once that summary follows, then stop notifying.

In criticism/interpretation, avoid slipping into plot summary. Use only enough summary to support analysis.

### Rule 18. Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end.

End position carries emphasis; often put new/logical predicate there.

| Original | Revision |
| --- | --- |
| Humanity has hardly advanced in fortitude since that time, though it has advanced in many other ways. | Humanity, since that time, has advanced in many other ways, but it has hardly advanced in fortitude. |
| This steel is principally used for making razors, because of its hardness. | Because of its hardness, this steel is principally used in making razors. |

Periodic sentence gains force by holding main statement to end. Beginning position can also be emphatic:

- Deceit or treachery he could never forgive.
- Through the middle of the valley flowed a winding stream.

Principle applies to sentence words, paragraph sentences, and composition paragraphs.

## V. Words And Expressions Commonly Misused

Use this section as quick usage lookup. Some forms are wrong; others are overused or vague. If substitution patches awkwardly, recast sentence.

**All right.** Familiar speech for "Agreed" / "Go ahead." Avoid elsewhere. Always two words.

**As good or better than.** Recast: *as good as his, or better*.

**As to whether.** Use *whether*.

**Bid.** Takes infinitive without *to*. Past tense meaning "ordered" = *bade*.

**But.** Unnecessary after *doubt* and *help*: *I have no doubt that*; *He could not help seeing that*. Avoid too many *but* loose sentences. Avoid *but... But* contrast chains; rearrange.

**Can.** Means able. Do not substitute for *may*.

**Case.** Often needless: *In many cases, the rooms...* -> *Many rooms...*; *It has rarely been the case...* -> *Few mistakes...*

**Certainly.** Overused intensifier, worse in writing than speech.

**Character.** Often redundant: *acts of a hostile character* -> *hostile acts*.

**Claim, vb.** Means lay claim to. With clause, use only when that sense fits. Not substitute for *declare*, *maintain*, *charge*.

**Clever.** Overused; restrict to ingenuity in small matters.

**Compare.** *Compare to* = point out resemblance between unlike things. *Compare with* = mainly differences between same-order things.

**Consider.** Not followed by *as* when meaning "believe to be": *I consider him competent.* Use *as* when meaning "examined as": *considered Cromwell as soldier and administrator*.

**Data.** Plural: *These data were tabulated.*

**Dependable.** Prefer *reliable* or *trustworthy*.

**Different than.** Use *different from*, *other than*, or *unlike*.

**Divided into.** Do not misuse for *composed of*. Plays divided into acts; poems composed of stanzas.

**Don't.** Contraction of *do not*. *Does not* = *doesn't*.

**Due to.** Do not use adverbially for *because of*: not *He lost, due to carelessness.* Correct when tied to noun/predicate: *This invention is due to Edison*; *losses due to preventable fires*.

**Folk.** Collective noun equivalent to *people*. Use singular form only.

**Effect.** Noun = result. Verb = bring about/accomplish. Do not confuse with *affect* = influence. Avoid vague art/fashion phrases like *a charming effect was produced by*.

**Etc.** Means *and the rest*. Use only when omitted items are obvious/immaterial. Incorrect after *such as* / *for example* list.

**Fact.** Use for directly verifiable matters, not judgments. See Rule 13 for *the fact that*.

**Factor.** Hackneyed; replace with direct phrasing: *His superior training was the great factor...* -> *He won by being better trained.*

**Feature.** Usually adds nothing. Tell what happened instead of saying it was a feature. Avoid advertising verb *feature*.

**Fix.** In writing, restrict to literary senses: fasten, make firm/immovable. Colloquial for arrange/prepare/mend.

**Get.** Avoid colloquial *have got* for *have*. Preferred participle: *got*.

**He is a man who.** Redundant; see Rule 13. *He is very ambitious.* / *I have always wanted to visit Spain.*

**Help.** See **But**.

**However.** Meaning *nevertheless*, do not put first in sentence/clause. Use: *At last, however, we succeeded.* At first means *in whatever way/to whatever extent*: *However you advise him...*

**Interesting.** Avoid as perfunctory introduction. Do not announce interest; make material interesting.

**Kind of.** Do not use for *rather* before adjectives/verbs or for *something like* before nouns except familiar style. Literal only. Same for *sort of*.

**Less.** Do not use for *fewer*. *Less* = quantity; *fewer* = number. Exception: round number as amount, e.g. *less than a hundred*.

**Like.** Do not use for *as* before phrases/clauses. *Like* governs nouns/pronouns: *He thought as I did* / *like me*.

**Line, along these lines.** Overworked for course/procedure/thought. Prefer direct phrasing: *He is studying French literature.*

**Literal, literally.** Do not use to intensify exaggeration/metaphor: *a literal flood of abuse* -> *a flood of abuse*.

**Lose out.** Weaker than *lose*. Same caution for *try out*, *win out*, *sign up*, *register up* unless idiomatic particle changes meaning.

**Most.** Not for *almost*: *Almost everybody*, *almost all the time*.

**Nature.** Often redundant like *character*: *hostile acts*. Vague in *lover of nature* unless specifics follow.

**Near by.** Adverbial phrase, not fully accepted; *near* / *near at hand* often better. As adjective, use *neighboring*.

**Oftentimes, ofttimes.** Archaic. Use *often*.

**One hundred and one.** Keep *and* in this and similar expressions.

**One of the most.** Threadbare opening formula. Also use plural verb in following relative clause: *one of the ablest men that have attacked this problem*.

**Participle for verbal noun.** Prefer possessive before gerund when subject of action immediately precedes it: *Do you mind my asking a question?* Recast if possessive becomes clumsy: *If it should become necessary to reconsider the whole matter*.

**People.** *The people* is political; do not confuse with *the public*. People give political support/opposition; public gives appreciation/patronage.

**Phase.** Stage of transition/development; not *aspect* or *topic*.

**Possess.** Do not use as fancy *have*/*own*: *He had great courage* / *He owned...*

**Prove.** Past participle = *proved*.

**Respective, respectively.** Usually omit: *works listed under the names of their authors*. Use only when needed for exact pairing/formal proof.

**Shall, Will.** Future: first person *shall*, second/third *will*. *I will* expresses determination/consent; *I shall* simple future.

**Should.** See **Would**.

**So.** Avoid as intensifier in writing: *so good*, *so warm*. For clauses, see Rule 4.

**Sort of.** See **Kind of**.

**Split Infinitive.** Has precedent, but disfavored by many careful writers. Prefer *to inquire diligently* over *to diligently inquire*.

**State.** Not fancy *say*/*remark*. Use for "express fully or clearly": *He refused to state his objections.*

**Student Body.** Usually means *students*: *a student*, *liked by the students*, *students passed resolutions*.

**System.** Often needless: *government by commission*; *dormitories*.

**Thanking You in Advance.** Sounds presumptuous. Make request politely; send acknowledgment later if needed.

**They.** Traditional rule: singular distributives (*each*, *everybody*, *anyone*, etc.) take singular pronoun; Strunk says use *he* unless feminine. Modern house style may prefer singular *they*; be consistent.

**Very.** Use sparingly; prefer strong words.

**Viewpoint.** Write *point of view*. Do not misuse for *view* or *opinion*.

**While.** Use mainly for "during the time that." Avoid for *and*/*but*; often semicolon is clearer. Use as *although* only when no ambiguity.

**Whom.** Often wrongly used for *who* before *he said* or similar when pronoun is subject of following verb: *His brother, who he said would send him the money*.

**Worth while.** Strictly for actions: *Is it worth while to telegraph?* Avoid vague approval/disapproval; never adjective before noun (*a worth while story*).

**Would.** First-person conditional takes *should*: *I should not have succeeded without his help.* Indirect future after past-tense verb: *he predicted that we should...* For habitual past, simple past is usually stronger: *Once a year he visited the old mansion.*
